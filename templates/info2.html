How to Spot Phishing Emails


Attackers often impersonate real companies or coworkers, but the email address may not give them away

  • Example: Misspelled Domains (e.g., "paypa1.com" instead of "paypal.com")
  • Example: Extra words (e.g, "paypal-security-team.net")
  • Example: Wrong top-level domains (e.g., ".info, .site, .xyz instead of .edu or .gov")

Phishing emails often use alarming language or threats to pressure you into acting quickly without thinking.

  • Example: "Your account will be suspended unless you verify your information immediately!"
  • Example: "Your package delivery has failed. Click here to reschedule!"
  • Example: "We detected suspicious activity on your account. Please log in to secure it!"

Hover over links to see the actual URL before clicking. Phishing emails often use URLs that look similar to legitimate ones but may have slight misspellings or additional characters.

  • Example: "https://www.paypa1.com" instead of "https://www.paypal.com"
  • Example: "https://www.amazon-secure.com" instead of "https://www.amazon.com"
  • Example: "https://www.apple-support.com" instead of "https://www.apple.com"

Legitimate organizations will never ask you to provide sensitive information (like passwords, credit card numbers, or social security numbers) via email. If you receive such a request, it's likely a phishing attempt.

  • Example: "Please reply with your password to verify your account."
  • Example: "Click this link and enter your credit card information to receive a refund."
  • Example: "We need your social security number to process your application. Please fill out the attached form."

Phishing emails often have a tone that doesn't match the organization's usual communication style. They may be overly formal, too casual, or contain spelling and grammar errors.

  • Example: "Dear Valued Customer, We are writing to inform you of a security breach on your account. Please click the link below to secure your account immediately."
  • Example: "Hey there! We noticed some weird activity on your account. Can you click this link and check it out?"
  • Example: "Congratulations! You've won a free iPhone! Click here to claim your prize!"
Real Email Phishing Email
Comes from a verified, correctly spelled domain (e.g., @university.edu). Uses look-alike or misspelled domains (e.g., @university-it-support.com).
Calm, professional tone with no pressure. Creates urgency or fear (“Your account will be closed today”).
Links match the organization's real website. Links redirect to unknown or suspicious domains.
Never asks for passwords or sensitive information. Requests login credentials, MFA codes, or personal data.
Expected communication based on your role or recent activity. Unexpected or out-of-context messages (“Invoice attached” when you don't handle invoices).
Proper grammar, formatting, and consistent branding. Odd phrasing, formatting issues, or inconsistent branding.
Attachments are expected and relevant. Unsolicited attachments, especially ZIP, EXE, or HTML files.

If you think an email might be a phishing attempt:

  • Don't click on any links or download attachments.
  • Don't reply to the email.
  • Report it to your IT department or the organization directly.
  • Delete the email from your inbox.
